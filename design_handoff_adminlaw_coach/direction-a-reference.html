When is a discretion legally unreasonable?

A decision may be set aside as legally unreasonable where it lacks an evident and intelligible justification. The test asks whether no reasonable decision-maker could have reached it.

When is an exercise of statutory discretion legally unreasonable? Ask
Answer

An exercise of statutory discretion may be set aside as legally unreasonable where it lacks an evident and intelligible justification. The orthodox standard asks whether the decision is one that no reasonable decision-maker could have arrived at.

Unreasonableness need not rest on a specific identified error. It may be an inference drawn from the decision itself — including from an outcome that is disproportionate or otherwise unjustified on the material before the decision-maker.
